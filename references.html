Endangered Species

Explore information about endangered bird species and their global distribution.

1. California Condor

The California Condor is a critically endangered species with a wingspan of up to 3 meters. It is primarily found in North America, with populations concentrated in California and Arizona.

Global Distribution:North America - Concentrated in California and Arizona

2. Javan Hawk-Eagle

The Javan Hawk-Eagle is a raptor species native to Java, Indonesia. Deforestation and habitat loss have led to a decline in their population, making them critically endangered.

Global Distribution:Java, Indonesia

3. Philippine Eagle

The Philippine Eagle, also known as the Monkey-Eating Eagle, is one of the largest and rarest eagles in the world. It is endemic to the Philippines and faces threats due to habitat destruction.

Global Distribution:Philippines

4. Kakapo

The Kakapo, or Night Parrot, is a critically endangered species native to New Zealand. It is a nocturnal, flightless parrot known for its unique behavior. Conservation efforts aim to save this rare bird from extinction.

Global Distribution:New Zealand

5. Northern Bald Ibis

The Northern Bald Ibis is a migratory bird species with a distinctive bald head. It faces threats from habitat loss and has a limited range in the Middle East and North Africa.

Global Distribution:Middle East and North Africa

7. Scarlet Macaw

The Scarlet Macaw (Ara macao) is a vibrant and colorful parrot species native to the rainforests of Central and South America. Known for its striking red, yellow, and blue plumage, the Scarlet Macaw faces threats such as habitat loss and illegal wildlife trade. Conservation efforts focus on protecting its natural habitat and implementing measures to combat illegal trafficking.

Global Distribution: Central and South America, including countries like Mexico, Peru, and Brazil.

6. Spoon-billed Sandpiper

The Spoon-billed Sandpiper (Calidris pygmaea) is a small wader bird known for its distinctive spoon-shaped bill. This species breeds in northeastern Russia and migrates to Southeast Asia during the non-breeding season. Rapid habitat loss in its breeding and wintering grounds, along with climate change, poses significant threats to the Spoon-billed Sandpiper's survival. Conservation efforts focus on protecting critical stopover sites and raising awareness about its plight.

Global Distribution: Breeds in northeastern Russia, migrates through countries like China, Japan, and stops in Southeast Asian countries during migration.
